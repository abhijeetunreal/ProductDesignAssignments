This project investigates the cultural and symbolic meanings attached to tea across various social classes—from affluent consumers who favor premium, artisanal blends to lower-income groups who rely on mass-produced or locally sourced tea. So far, the project has been structured through a comprehensive literature review, development of a semi-structured interview guide, and a pilot study. Preliminary interviews and focus groups have been conducted to refine research questions, ensuring the approach captures both overt usage patterns and deeper semiotic interpretations.

Context:

Tea is a universally consumed beverage that carries a spectrum of meanings and cultural significances. For the affluent, tea may represent a refined lifestyle, an element of a health-conscious regimen, or even a symbol of luxury when sourced from boutique tea houses. For middle- and lower-income consumers, tea can be a daily comfort, an affordable means of social connection, or a tradition steeped in cultural heritage. This research explores how different social classes interpret tea—not only as a beverage but as a carrier of lifestyle, identity, and socio-economic status.

Approach Details:

  Semi-Structured Interviews: Develop an interview guide with open-ended questions aimed at uncovering why individuals choose specific types of tea, how they source it, and what meanings they attach to its consumption.

  Focus Groups: Organize group discussions to observe interactions and the negotiation of meaning among peers. This reveals shared cultural narratives and variations in perception across different classes.

  Observational Research: Visit various tea-selling venues—from upscale specialty tea shops to local grocery stores and street vendors—to understand the environmental context and presentation of tea as a product.

Data Collection Techniques:

  Interviews: In-depth, one-on-one interviews with participants selected using a stratified sampling method to ensure representation from super-rich, middle-income, and super-poor groups.

  Focus Groups: Group discussions in community centers, tea lounges, and local markets where tea is commonly purchased.

  Observational Fieldwork: Notes and photographs (with consent) from visits to different tea outlets, including high-end boutiques and everyday retail environments.

Data Recording:
Interviews and focus groups are audio-recorded (with permission) and later transcribed for thematic coding. Observational data is captured through detailed field notes, which document not only the product presentation but also the context and ambiance of the tea consumption environment.
